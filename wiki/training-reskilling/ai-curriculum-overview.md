# AI Curriculum for the Underserved and Overlooked

StepUp.One's core training offering is a progressive AI skills curriculum designed to take anyone -- including people with zero technical background -- from basic digital literacy to building real applications and platforms. The curriculum has no contracts, no fees, and no prerequisites.

## Philosophy

The curriculum rests on one belief: AI is the great equalizer. A four-year-old can build a website using only their voice. A refugee who has never coded can produce social media content, raise capital, or build applications. The technology StepUp.One's team uses is better than what 90% of London startups use -- the gap is not access to tools but access to teaching.

Mohamed Anis frames it as: "I don't care what you produce as long as you produce it through AI." The curriculum is tool-agnostic and outcome-focused.

## Progressive Skill Ladder

The curriculum is structured as a progressive ladder, starting with the lowest-friction skill and ascending:

1. **Website creation using AI** -- a five-minute task, voice-driven, produces a site better than most hand-coded efforts. This is the entry point because everyone wants a website for something.
2. **Social media post creation** -- using AI to generate content for LinkedIn, Instagram, and other platforms.
3. **Blog creation** -- longer-form content, SEO-oriented, for Google visibility.
4. **Capital raising** -- using AI to prepare pitch decks, investor outreach, financial models.
5. **Music creation** -- AI-generated music for those with creative interests.
6. **Application and platform building** -- the highest level, where participants become builders, not just users.

At each level, participants can also be helped to land jobs, gigs, and freelance work related to that skill -- website development, social media management, sales, marketing, capital advisory.

## Delivery Model

- **Daily Learning sessions** run throughout the day at various times, allowing participants to join at a time that works for them.
- Sessions are live, interactive, and taught by Mohamed Anis and senior team members.
- Participants learn by doing -- the sessions demonstrate AI tool usage in real time.
- There is no repetition of material; the sessions move like a train, and participants must keep up or catch up independently.
- Beginners who need fundamental support are separated into dedicated short sessions to avoid slowing the main group.

## Target Populations

The curriculum is designed for:

- **Post-release populations** -- people recently released from prison who have internet access and need employable skills. This was identified as the most accessible group in the Netherlands through the collaboration with [Rivelino Rigters](../entities/people/rivelino-rigters.md).
- **Refugees in camps** -- limited by internet restrictions; StepUp.One often must secure jobs and send money directly because the camps are "locked worlds."
- **Youth with trauma backgrounds** -- people who have been "broken by life, circumstances, or mental issues" and struggle with motivation and appointments. The curriculum's progressive, no-pressure structure is designed to accommodate uneven attendance.
- **Anyone from underserved communities** -- no geographic or demographic restriction.

## The AI + Human Model

A critical principle: AI builds the technology component, but the human provides the talent that makes people pay. Every skill in the curriculum pairs AI capability with human judgment:

- AI can generate a website in minutes, but a human decides what problem the website solves.
- AI can draft outreach messages, but a human understands the client's desperate needs.
- AI can create music, but a human brings the creative vision.

The goal is not to make people dependent on AI tools, but to make them builders who combine AI with their own talents to deliver "tremendous value" that others will pay for.

## Critique of Alternative Approaches

Mohamed Anis is direct about ideas that don't serve the underserved well. When [Rivelino Rigters](../entities/people/rivelino-rigters.md) described a speaker refurbishment project (installing Bluetooth plates in old speakers), Mohamed Anis called it a "rich man's idea" -- competing with Chinese mass production for thin margins doesn't help thousands of people. The curriculum always asks: can this idea scale to help thousands of people in a clean way?
