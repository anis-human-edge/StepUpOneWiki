# Authority in Sales

One of the most important and most difficult lessons in StepUp.One's training: sales professionals must project authority, not desperation. This principle is taught repeatedly in Daily Learning sessions because it runs counter to the natural instinct of people from underserved backgrounds.

## The Core Problem

When team members get a client response, their instinct is to immediately offer everything: a full service breakdown, a CEO meeting, multiple links, pricing, and a sign-up page. This overwhelms the client and signals desperation.

Mohamed Anis's diagnosis: "Right now I see Mahmud as a beggar. He looks like a beggar to me." The irony is that Mahmud's words were "high-fi" and polished -- but the behavior pattern (offering everything at once) communicated neediness.

## The Anti-Pattern: What Begging Looks Like

Real examples from sessions:

- Client asks "tell me more about your service" and the team member responds with a full breakdown, a link to book a meeting with the CEO, pricing, and multiple resources -- all in one message.
- Client says "I'm interested" and the team member sends the education message and the convince message back-to-back, without waiting for a response.
- Team member follows up by adding more and more information each time the client goes silent.

The result: clients sense desperation, build a "security mechanism," and slowly disengage. "Even if they say thank you, they're walking you to the door."

## The Principle: Small Responses, Let Them Ask

The correct approach:

1. Give a brief, confident response. Stop.
2. Let the client ask for more.
3. Give a brief answer to what they asked. Stop again.
4. Repeat until the client is genuinely pursuing you.

Example exchange:
- "What do you do?" -- "I'm building an AI-native system for go-to-market." Stop.
- "Really? What do you mean?" -- Brief explanation. Stop.
- "That sounds interesting, how much?" -- Now you have a real prospect.

## The Proof: Michael Alf

Mohamed Anis demonstrated the principle with a real client story. Michael Alf came to a meeting and was "quite arrogant." Mohamed Anis's internal posture: "I don't care about you. What I have is extremely important. If you beg me, I will give it to you."

Forty minutes in, Mohamed Anis ended the meeting: "Wow, amazing meeting. Thank you." And closed. Normal beggars would hang on, hoping for crumbs.

Result: Michael Alf started chasing Mohamed Anis -- WhatsApp messages, emails, follow-ups, asking "Why are you not responding?"

## Flexing with References

When a client asks for proof (white paper, customer reference), the authority approach is:

1. Provide the white paper directly. "Here is our paper."
2. Offer a reference that is dramatically bigger than the client's company. Mohamed Anis gave Dominic (a DevOps company founder) a reference to Richard at Dodo -- a company "100 times bigger" than Dominic's.
3. End with a challenge, not a plea: "Let me know how much you understood."

Farah Ibrahim named this "flexing" -- providing proof that simultaneously establishes your scale.

## The Difficulty Acknowledgment

Mohamed Anis is explicit that this is hard: "I'm asking you to do something extremely difficult. I know what I'm asking you to do is very very difficult. You have never been a billionaire."

He shares his own history: "The first 10 meetings I did, I was begging just like you guys. My heart was pumping a thousand times per minute." The change came not from pretending, but from genuinely believing in the value of what he had.

The tool is the bridge: "Even if you are a beggar, the tool you're holding in your hand is a billion-dollar thing."

## Farah Ibrahim's Test

Farah Ibrahim asked the team: "Is there anybody in StepUp.One who begged and got a client?" The answer was no. Begging has never worked. The evidence supports authority.
