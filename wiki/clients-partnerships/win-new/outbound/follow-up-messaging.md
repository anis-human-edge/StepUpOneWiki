# Follow-up Messaging Strategy

Follow-up is where most outbound deals are won or lost. StepUp.One's approach combines persistence with authority -- following up aggressively while never appearing desperate.

## When to Follow Up

- After an education message with no response: wait 3 days minimum, then send a one-line follow-up.
- After a price quote with no response: follow up, but do not re-educate or re-convince. Ask a direct question.
- After a meeting that went well but the client went silent: persistent follow-up with an authoritative tone.
- After months of silence (e.g., the David client, 6-12 months): still worth following up. "Just keep following up until he says yes or no."

## Message Length and Format

There is an active debate within the team about message length:

- **Mohamed sharmuge Mohamed's position**: long follow-up messages deter recipients. "He will never have the time to read all these things."
- **Patience Ciza's position**: long messages convey a "strong message" and are worth sending.
- **Mohamed Anis's resolution**: concise is almost always better for initial outreach and follow-ups. "Choose concise for all your posts. Standard or detailed is too much information."

The recommended format: **one-line follow-up messages**. Generate them in the AI studio, submit for review, then send.

## Tone and Attitude

Follow-up messages must project authority, not desperation:

- **Talk as if you're their boss.** "Should we close the door or is there anything?" is better than "Just checking in, would you like to schedule a call?"
- **Never re-send everything.** If the client got the education message, don't send it again with a convince message stacked on top.
- **Use inbound tactics alongside outbound.** When a client is not responding to messages, comment on their recent LinkedIn post. This creates a parallel touch point without adding desperation to the message thread.

Example from Patience Ciza: a client posted 18 hours ago but hadn't responded to messages. The strategy: comment on the post first, then send the follow-up message. This ensures the team's name appears in two contexts -- as a thoughtful commenter and as a direct message.

## The Education-to-Convince Pipeline

Messages flow through stages:

1. **Connect** -- initial outreach, one sentence
2. **Educate** -- share what you do and why it matters
3. **Convince** -- provide proof (white paper, client references, results)
4. **Follow-up** -- if the client goes silent at any stage
5. **Close** -- book the meeting or get a definitive answer

Critical rule: do not send education and convince messages back-to-back. Send one, wait for a response or reasonable time, then send the next. Stacking them signals desperation.

Libaan ali had 9 opportunities stuck in the "Educate" stage since February. The instruction: send the convince message now and move them to Gold or Bronze status.

## CRM and Pipeline Tracking

- Each follow-up interaction should be tracked in the CRM.
- Opportunities should be categorized by stage (Educate, Gold, Bronze) and by motion (win-new, serve-current, grow-existing, win-back).
- A client can have multiple opportunities across motions -- they are separate threads with different objectives.
- Lost clients being re-engaged should acknowledge the initial failure and lead with new results from other clients.

## AI-Human Partnership in Follow-ups

The AI generates initial drafts, but human refinement is critical:

- AI messages can be "sophisticated" and "aggressive" but sometimes miss the mark on specific client context.
- When a client asks for a white paper, the AI might respond with "No white paper. We build pipelines, not PDFs." -- which has the right attitude but may need human judgment about when to use it.
- Best practice: let AI draft the message, then have a human refine it. "You can even give this as a copy and say: hey, this is my response, make it better."
