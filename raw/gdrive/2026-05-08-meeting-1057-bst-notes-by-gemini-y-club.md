### Details

- **Critical Thinking Curriculum Design**: Mohamed Anis explains that the program must teach first and then introduce an AI companion. The challenge is not just knowing how to use ChatGPT — it's being able to digest the second-order thoughts the companion generates. People can use the tool but can't process what it produces.

- **Importance of Critical Thinking**: Mohamed Anis originally planned a book on 21st-century skills, realized critical thinking was the #1 skill. His daughter's critical thinking course was found lacking — no clear structure, typical "keep talking" American teaching style. Hypothesis: live classes may not teach concepts but rather engage students enough to want to return.

- **Reframing Complex Concepts**: Debanjana Paul suggested: instead of calling it "second-order thinking," use "short-term effect / long-term effect." Example: palm oil helps the economy immediately (first order) but destroys the rainforest long-term (second order). This reframing makes it accessible.

- **Y Club Product Overview**: A multi-level approach:
  - **Level 1** (defensive critical thinking — "spot the trick"): Free, done with parents, structured like a game with badges. For 9-12 year olds. Y Engine 1.0. 20 badges covering concepts like "who wins," "evidence hunter," "bias spotter," escalating to: "are the numbers being used fairly?", "is this an argument or a trick?", "can I make the other side stronger first?" Core goal: reach 1 million kids with parents.
  - **Level 2** (offensive critical thinking — "run the game"): 24-week program, self-study with AI companion. Y Engine 2.4. Paid. 6 modules: Hack Yourself, Build Your Toolkit, Bet Smart, Master the System, See the System, Apply to Real Decision. For ages 10-15.
  - **Level 3** (real world mastery): Cohort with live teacher. Allows interaction with users to collect feedback and improve Levels 1 and 2.

- **Launch Goal**: Get Level 1 in front of 1 million parents within 3 months. The product spreads virally if compelling — light one WhatsApp channel and parents will share it. The problem to solve: how to make Level 1 so compelling that the first lesson hooks them.

- **Target Customer**: The product is sold to parents, not children. Parents who understand: "Every child alive today will grow up with AI. Most will let AI think for them before they learn to think for themselves. There is one skill that decides whether a kid uses AI or gets used by it: critical thinking."

- **Corporate Application**: Debanjana Paul suggested applying the model to corporate workers who rely on AI stupidly. Mohamed Anis acknowledged: critical thinking for IT services is different from telecom is different from politicians. Level 1 (free) can be a Trojan horse — give it to all employees, then Level 2 has a small payment.

- **Freemium Analogy**: Like ChatGPT and Claude free tiers — free users are "market share," but the real value is in paid. Level 1 free → Level 2 paid. Same pattern.

- **Division of Work**: Mohamed Anis continues building Level 2. Debanjana Paul focuses on: how to make Level 1 so engaging that real parents and real children consume and love it. Provides feedback by end of weekend. Launch timeline: 3-4 weeks.
